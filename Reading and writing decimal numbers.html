Reading and Writing Decimal Numbers

1. The Price Tag Panic! (Problem to Solve) 🏷️

PROBLEM SITUATION 😳

Ada goes to a supermarket in Lagos and sees a box of biscuits with a price tag that says ₦450.75. The cashier asks her, "How much is that?" Ada freezes. She knows it's "four hundred and fifty Naira," but what about the ".75" part? How do you say that correctly? 🤔

Have you ever felt like Ada? That little dot, the decimal point, can be tricky. But by the end of this lesson, you'll be able to read and write any decimal number with confidence, from prices in a shop to measurements in a science lab! Let's decode the dot! 🔍

2. Decimals All Around You in Nigeria 🇳🇬

Decimals are not just in your maths textbook; they are everywhere! Here are a few places you'll see them every day.

Money, Money, Money! 💰

This is the most common one! Prices like ₦500.50 (Five hundred Naira, fifty kobo) or a bank balance of ₦10,254.25 are decimal numbers.

At the Petrol Station ⛽

When your parents buy fuel, the pump shows the price per litre, like ₦617.70, and the amount of fuel pumped, like 35.5 litres. Both are decimal numbers!

Sports and Athletics 🏃‍♀️

When watching the Olympics, a sprinter's time might be 9.58 seconds. That decimal shows a fraction of a second, which makes the difference between gold and silver!

Science and Measurement 🔬

In your science class, you might measure the length of a leaf as 7.5 cm or the weight of a chemical as 12.25 grams. Decimals allow for precise measurements.

Technology (Radio & Phones) 📻

When you tune a radio to a station like 96.9 FM, you are using a decimal. Even the version of software on a phone can be something like Android 13.1.

3. Your Decimal Dictionary 📖

To master decimals, we need to know these four important terms. They are the keys to understanding everything!

Decimal Point

The dot (.) in a decimal number. Its job is to be the great separator! It separates the whole numbers on the left from the fractional parts on the right.

Place Value

The value of a digit based on its position in the number. In decimals, the positions to the right of the decimal point have special names that end in "-ths".

Tenths

The first place to the right of the decimal point. It represents a fraction out of 10. For example, in 0.7, the 7 is in the tenths place (⁷/₁₀).

Hundredths

The second place to the right of the decimal point. It represents a fraction out of 100. For example, in 0.03, the 3 is in the hundredths place (³/₁₀₀).

4. The Golden Rules for Reading Decimals 🏆

Let's learn how to read the number 24.38 using a place value chart. It's easy when you follow these rules!

Tens Ones . Tenths Hundredths
2 4 . 3 8
1

Read the Whole Number Part

Read the number to the left of the decimal point just like a normal number.

For 24.38, we say "Twenty-four".

2

Say "and" for the Decimal Point

When you get to the decimal point (.), you say the word "and".

So far, we have "Twenty-four and..."

Why "and"? This is the proper mathematical way. It clearly separates the whole part from the fraction part.

3

Read the Part After the Point

Read the number to the right of the decimal point as if it were a whole number.

For 24.38, we say "thirty-eight".

4

Say the Place Value of the LAST Digit

Look at the very last digit (8). What place value is it in? It's in the Hundredths place. So, you end by saying that place value name.

So we have "...thirty-eight hundredths".

Why the last digit? The place of the last digit names the entire fractional part. Because the 8 is in the hundredths place, it means we have 38 out of 100 parts (³⁸/₁₀₀).

Put it all together: Twenty-four and thirty-eight hundredths.

5. Case Study: Kunle's Science Project 🌿

Kunle is doing a project on plant growth for his JSS 1 science class. He measures the length of a new leaf with a very precise ruler. The ruler shows the length is 5.4 cm. He needs to write this measurement down in words for his report.

How should Kunle write 5.4 in words?

Here's how Kunle writes it:

Step 1: Whole Number Part. The number to the left of the dot is 5. He writes "Five".

Step 2: Decimal Point. The dot means he writes "and".

Step 3: Part After Point. The number to the right of the dot is 4. He thinks of the word "four".

Step 4: Place Value of Last Digit. The digit 4 is the last (and only) digit after the point. It is in the Tenths place.

✅ Final Answer: Kunle writes down "Five and four tenths".

6. Financial Focus: Reading a Receipt 🧾

Folake's mum sends her to a local supermarket to buy three items. The receipt shows the following prices:

  • Bread: ₦950.50
  • Milk: ₦1240.25
  • Sugar: ₦800.75

To practise her maths, Folake decides to write the price of the Milk in words on the back of the receipt. How should she write ₦1240.25?

Let's write out the price:

1. The Whole Number (Naira): The number to the left is 1240. We write "One thousand, two hundred and forty Naira".

2. The Decimal Point: We say "and".

3. The Part After (Kobo): The number to the right is 25. We think of "twenty-five".

4. The Place Value: The last digit, 5, is in the hundredths place. So we say "hundredths". (In money, this represents kobo!)

✅ Final Answer: "One thousand, two hundred and forty and twenty-five hundredths". Or, when speaking about money, we would simply say, "One thousand, two hundred and forty Naira, twenty-five kobo."

7. Maths Magic: The Money Trick! 💰

Think in Naira and Kobo

Having trouble with tenths and hundredths? Think about money! 🇳🇬

Imagine the number is a price in Naira. The first two places after the decimal point are just like Kobo.

  • 0.1 is like 10 Kobo. It's one-tenth of a Naira.
  • 0.7 is like 70 Kobo. It's seven-tenths of a Naira.
  • 0.01 is like 1 Kobo. It's one-hundredth of a Naira.
  • 0.45 is like 45 Kobo. It's forty-five-hundredths of a Naira.

This trick makes it easy to remember that the first place is tenths and the second is hundredths!

Quick Warning!

This trick is great for the first two decimal places. Remember that decimals can keep going! The third place is "thousandths," the fourth is "ten-thousandths," and so on. The money trick won't help you there, but it's perfect for most everyday numbers!

8. Watch Out! Avoid These Decimal Traps! ⚠️

Mistake: Saying "Point" Instead of "And"

While people might understand you if you say "two point five," the correct mathematical way to read it is using "and."

❌ Wrong Way

Reading 2.5 as "Two point five".

Reading 10.12 as "Ten point one two".

✅ Right Way

Reading 2.5 as "Two and five tenths".

Reading 10.12 as "Ten and twelve hundredths".

Mistake: Forgetting the "-ths" Suffix

The places after the decimal point sound similar to whole numbers, but they MUST end in "-ths" to show they are fractions.

❌ Wrong Way

Reading 0.04 as "Four hundred". This is a big number!

✅ Right Way

Reading 0.04 as "Four hundredths". This is a tiny fraction!

9. Practice & Challenge Zone 🧠

Standard Practice Problem

Write the decimal number 152.09 in words.

Hint: Follow the 4 Golden Rules. Remember to say "and" for the dot. The last digit, 9, is in which place value? Tenths or Hundredths?

Step 1 (Whole): One hundred fifty-two

Step 2 (Dot): and

Step 3 (Part): nine

Step 4 (Place Value): The 9 is in the hundredths place.

✅ Final Answer: One hundred fifty-two and nine hundredths.

⭐ Challenge Problem ⭐

Write the following number in numerals (digits):

"Four hundred six and seventy-three thousandths"

Hint: Work backwards. "Four hundred six" is the whole part. "Seventy-three" is the number after the dot. The word "thousandths" means the very last digit (the 3) must be in the third decimal place. What do you need to put in the tenths place?

1. Whole Part: "Four hundred six" is 406.

2. "And": This means we put a decimal point: 406.

3. The Fractional Part: We need to write "seventy-three thousandths". The word "thousandths" tells us the number must end in the 3rd decimal place.

To make the digit '3' of '73' end in the 3rd place, we must write 073 after the decimal point.

✅ Final Answer: 406.073

10. Your Golden Rules for Reading Decimals 📜

If you get stuck, just remember these four simple rules.

  1. Read the Left: Read the whole number part to the left of the dot. ⬅️
  2. Say "AND": Say the word "and" for the decimal point. 🗣️
  3. Read the Right: Read the entire number part to the right of the dot. ➡️
  4. Name the Place: Say the place value name of the very last digit (e.g., tenths, hundredths, thousandths). 📍
